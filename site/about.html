About CyberFuturo

Mission

Publish the canonical, free, rigorously documented set of leading indicators for how fast the digital, compute, and energy foundations of the near future are actually being built.

Why it exists

Most near-future technology analysis is opinion dressed as insight — essays about which startup is going to "redefine" a category, which year "transforms everything," which technology is going to "win." Both the essays and the vendor-funded trend reports have well-known credibility failure modes. A reader interested in what is actually happening, as opposed to what a commentator thinks might happen, has surprisingly few places to look.

CyberFuturo is one of those places. We track a small set of rigorously defined indices using only free public data — arXiv, government datasets, SEC filings, open APIs. Every index has a documented query, a reproducible pipeline, and a methodology page you can audit. When a number moves, we publish the chart. We do not forecast. We do not interpret heavily. We let the data do the talking.

Our bet is simple: over enough time, measured truth compounds into authority, and opinion rots.

What CyberFuturo is

  • A free, every-other-week brief citing a small number of documented indices
  • A public, reproducible dataset with CSV and SVG outputs in a versioned repository
  • A versioned methodology page for every published index — with known limitations and deprecation criteria named explicitly
  • A publication that discloses AI-assistance in prose and excludes LLMs from the data pipeline entirely
  • A sponsor-supported publication where sponsors are directly sold, named, and never appear above the editorial

What CyberFuturo is not

Deliberate negative space — things we do not do, and will say no to even when asked.

  • Not a consulting firm. We do not do private research for fees. Custom index commissions are a separate product — a venture firm can pay us to publicly track a specific indicator, but the result is public and versioned like every other index.
  • Not a trading signal service. We do not provide investment advice, trading tips, or price targets.
  • Not a news aggregator. We do not summarize other publications. Every piece of content we publish is an original measurement or a brief built on one.
  • Not a general tech blog. We do not write about product launches, personal takes, or industry drama.
  • Not an AI-native publication. LLMs assist with prose, but no index, number, or headline is LLM-authored.
  • Not a tipping / donation product. Support the publication by subscribing, sharing an issue, or sponsoring a slot — not by dropping a tip.
  • Not programmatic ad inventory. No display ads, no sidebar banners, no tracking pixels, no affiliate walls.

How it is built

CyberFuturo is a static site deployed to Cloudflare Pages and a small set of Python scripts that run on a monthly schedule. The scripts use only Python stdlib — no third-party dependencies — and fetch data from public APIs. Charts are rendered as SVG text directly by the pipeline, so every chart in the repo is diff-able in git.

The architectural decisions behind every one of those choices are documented as Architecture Decision Records in the private repository, alongside a complete domain model. The shortlist:

  • No forecasts (ADR-0001)
  • Python stdlib only (ADR-0002)
  • Static site, no JavaScript framework (ADR-0003)
  • Cloudflare Pages via direct-upload deploy (ADR-0004)
  • Charts as in-pipeline SVG text (ADR-0005)
  • Private repo, public site (ADR-0006)
  • Versioned per-index methodology (ADR-0007)
  • LLMs never generate numbers (ADR-0008)
  • $0 free-tier-only infrastructure (ADR-0009)
  • Trailing-window time series, documented drift (ADR-0010)

Who runs it

A single human editor, working with AI-assisted prose tooling and a deterministic pipeline. One voice, one set of rules, one name on the line for every claim made.

Contact: reply to any brief or open an issue on the repo. Methodology errors and data disputes are welcomed; they become changelog entries in the next brief.

How to support it

  • Subscribe to the brief — that is the single most useful thing a reader can do.
  • Share an issue with one person who would find it valuable.
  • Sponsor a brief if your company targets deep-tech audiences. The editorial rules describe the constraints; reach out via the repo for rate card and format.
  • Report a bug if a number or methodology page has an error. Corrections are public and logged.
